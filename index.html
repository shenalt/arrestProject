Black and Hispanic People Were the Most Arrested Groups in New York City in 2024

Blacks and Hispanic people made up 83% of arrests made in New York City.

By: Shenal Tissera
December 14, 2024

New York City is home to the largest police force in the country, the New York Police Department. More than 33,000 don the uniform, with badge and gun in tow.

Of course, part of their job includes arresting folks throughout the five boroughs. Looking at arrest data from this year, there are some telling patterns that arise.

There were 196,449 arrests made in the city as of December 5 in 2024. Black people made up the highest frequency of arrested folk with 91,113 arrested in total. They were followed by Hispanics at 72,259 when combining white and black hispanics. White people came in third place with 19,629.

Already, there is quite a significant gap between the amount minorities, specifically black and Hispanic people, get arrested in the city. The racial breakdown of New York City shows an even greater divide in the way certain groups of people in New York City are policed compared to others.

White people make up 31% of the population of New York City whereas black and hispanic people make up 21% and 20.5% of the city as of 2022. Yet, black and Hispanic people made up 37% of arrests made in the city. White people made up 10% of all arrests made this year.

The planned Fiscal Year 2025 budget for the NYPD sits at $5.8 billion. A lot of money and resources goes into the police force. Seemingly, a lot of it goes into policing neighborhoods that are predominantly black and/or Hispanic.

For example, there is an abundance of arrests in neighborhoods like Brownsville and Corona which consist of black and Hispanic people respectively and unsurprisingly comprise of mainly black and Hispanic individuals respectively.

For underserved communities, a heightened police presence isn't conducive of positive change. Personally, living on Staten Island in the North Shore, I observe the obvious heavy police presence in my neighborhood, which consists of many black and Hispanic people. It is not surprising that the bulk of the arrests in Staten Island are in my area and not the more wealthy and white neighborhoods. Instead of increasing funding to public schools and providing supportive housing to homeless people in my community, there seems to be an emphasis on police who seem more like an occupying force rather than a force for good in the community.
